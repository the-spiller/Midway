Battle of Midway Game

Midway is is a two-player, turn-based, historical World War II simulation wargame. It recreates a landmark historical battle—very few have had similar impact on their war’s outcome. It gives players an idea of the challenges facing the commanders of the opposing fleets, yet without so much detail that it is difficult to understand or begin to play. This is something of a balancing act, and as a result some historical elements are overlooked (e.g. destroyers and submarines have been left out). The game system is not very original; it's been tweaked, but veteran board wargamers will recognize it immediately. Any changes we’ve made were in an effort to take better advantage of this medium without losing too much of its old-time map-and-counters feel.

Midway Atoll in November 1941

Each player takes a side: forces of the Imperial Japanese Navy Combined Fleet (IJN for short) commanded by Admiral Isoroku Yamamoto, or two task forces of the U.S. Navy Pacific Fleet (USN) under the overall command of Admiral Chester Nimitz. Players direct the movement and combat of the ships and aircraft actually present for the battle. The the historical object is control of Midway Atoll. It begins the game in U.S. hands and pays big dividends to the Japanese player if he or she can successfully capture it.

The game begins at 0500 3 June 1942 and is played in turns representing two hours (5 hours at night) through 1700 on 6 June—34 turns in all. The game ends when the last turn is completed, when one player withdraws all of his or her ships from the map, or when a player chooses to retire. The winner is determined by points given for control of Midway and destruction of enemy ships and aircraft.

At first glance, the game might appear to be a more complex version of Battleship, and that’s not far wrong. Most of the game is played on a map of the central Pacific around Midway Atoll where neither player can see the other's ships. The map has a grid of squares about 20 miles across that’s used to control movement and determine range. Players have a limited number of searches each turn they use by selecting grid locations, and when enemy ships are located they may be attacked.

Combat can occur in two ways. Usually it’s one player launching planes from carriers to attack his or her opponent from the air. Should ships of both players wind up sharing one square on the map grid, ship-to-ship surface combat may ensue, though this is quite unlikely.

The History

Background

On the morning of 4 June 1942, Imperial Japan was winning its new war with the United States. At nightfall, the Americans were winning. The battle would linger on for two more days, but the Japanese were forced back onto their heels and would stay there until the war ended in late summer 1945.

Imperial Japan, 1942

The war began with a Japanese surprise attack on the U.S. Navy base at Pearl Harbor six months before. The Imperial Japanese Navy’s aircraft carrier striking force Kido Butai launched about 350 warplanes that sank or crippled the battleships of the U.S. Navy’s Pacific Fleet, announcing with convincing violence that sea power now included air power. In one of those ironies that litter military history, the IJN’s carrier-based attack came while the U.S. fleet’s carriers and their escorting ships were safely away from Pearl.

Afterwards, while America scrambled to assemble men and matériel to fight this war, Japan's ground and naval forces rampaged across the western Pacific, seizing Burma, Thailand, French Indochina, British Malaya, the Philippines and more. They threatened India and Australia and provided a strong protective screen for what they deemed their “Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere.” Men of the Imperial Japanese Army and Navy believed themselves invincible, and not without reason. These were very dark days for the United States and its allies.

In April a bright spot for the U.S. emerged when a Pacific Fleet task force—including one of the aircraft carriers that the Pearl Harbor attack missed—snuck to within 650 nautical miles of the Japanese home islands and launched sixteen Army B-25 “Mitchell” medium bombers to attack Tokyo and other Japanese cities. History knows this as the Doolittle Raid for the man who planned and led it, Lt. Colonel Jimmy Doolittle. Little damage was done, but the raid accomplished its primary goal of raising American morale at a time when it was at its lowest. More, it embarassed and enraged Japanese military leaders—how dare the Americans threaten the life of the Emperor! And make me look like a fool!

The raid gave Admiral Isoroku Yamamoto just what he needed to sell an audacious idea to Japan's Naval General Staff: Operation MI. This was a plan to sieze the U.S. Navy base at tiny Midway Atoll, part of the Hawaiian Islands chain about 1100 nautical miles northwest of Oahu.
