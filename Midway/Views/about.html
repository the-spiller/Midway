The Game

Midway is is a two-player, turn-based, double-blind World War II historical simulation wargame. It is based on a landmark battle that occurred in early June, 1942 between forces of the United States Navy Pacific Fleet and the Imperial Japanese Navy Combined Fleet. It will give players an idea of the challenges that faced the commanders of the opposing fleets, but without so much detail that it is difficult to learn to play. This is a balancing act that results in some historical elements being overlooked—for example, destroyers and submarines have been left out of the game.

The game system is not particularly original; it's been tweaked, but veteran board wargamers will recognize it immediately.

Midway Atoll in November 1941

Each player takes a side: Japan’s forces commanded by Admiral Isoroku Yamamoto, or those of the United States under the command of Admiral Chester Nimitz. Players direct the movement and combat of the ships and aircraft actually present for the battle (except, of course, those destroyers and submarines mentioned above).

At first glance, the game might appear to be a more sophisticated version of Battleship, and that’s not far wrong. Most of the game is played on a map of the central Pacific around Midway Atoll on which each player sees only their own ships and airfields. The map has a square grid (about 20 miles to a side) that’s used to control movement and determine range. Players have a limited number of searches available each turn that they use by selecting grid locations. When enemy ships are located, only their types—battleship, aircraft carrier, cruiser, etc.—are revealed. No infomation on the number of each type present is given, nor anything about any specific ship.

The game is played in turns representing two hours in daylight or five hours at night. It begins at 0500 3 June 1942, and runs out 34 turns later at 1700 on 6 June. The game ends when this last turn is completed or when either player chooses to retire. The winner is determined by points given for control of Midway Atoll and the destruction of enemy ships.

Each turn is divided into phases: movement, search, air operations planning, air operations execution, Midway reduction, and surface combat (each is described in more detail below). In a given turn some phases might not happen; e.g. if neither player plans air operations, there will be no air operations execution phase.

Combat can occur in two ways. Usually it’s air operations attack missions: sending aircraft that are available and in range to attack a grid square containing enemy ships. Should ships of both players wind up sharing one square on the map grid, ship-to-ship surface combat ensues, but this is quite unusual (and never happened during the actual battle).

Registration

To register for the game, select the “I’d like to register” link on the login page.

On the Midway Registration page, submit an email address and a nickname. We’ll use your email address to send you a nasty, computer-generated password so you can log in. Your nickname is how you will be known in the game. Both your email address and nickname must be unique or they will be rejected.

Rest assured that other players can’t see your email address and that we’ll never give it away or spam you in any way. If you cancel your registration we’ll remove your email address from our records and forget that we ever had it.

If you lose or forget your password at any time (even if you’ve never used it), you can fill in your email address and click the “I forgot my password” link on the login page to have another nasty, computer-generated password sent to your email address.

When you log in using the password we send, you’ll arrive at Midway Home page. Click the Registration tab to edit your registration information (including changing your password to something you’ll be able to remember). Cancel your registration here by simply clicking on the “Cancel my registration” link and confiming that that’s what you want to do.

Starting a Game

On the Home page, players are presented with a list of the games that they are participating in. Select one and press the Play button to pick it up where it was left off.

To start a new game, select the “New Game” entry for the side you’d like to play. If there's a specific opponent you’r looking for, enter their nickname in the “Optional opponent nickname” text box. If you don’t enter a nickname, the first player seeking a new game on the other side will become your opponent. Click on the Play button to create the game and begin play. Once a new game is created, it is added to the list of available games and will appear there until completed.

Game Play

Victory

During the game points are awarded to the players as follows:

1. Starting with the 19th turn (0500 5 June) the U.S. player gets one point for each turn in which Midway is not taken by the Japanese player, for a possible total of 15.

Sunk, Enterprise is worth 10 points to the IJN player

2. The Japanese player receives 15 points for taking Midway before the game ends.

3. Ships are assigned point values, and players receive them when they sink an opponent’s ship. These point values are shown circled on ship images on the Search Map Arrivals and Zone tabs, and on the Battle Map.

When the game ends, the player with the most points has won!

The History

Background

On the morning of 4 June 1942, Imperial Japan was winning its war with the United States. By lunchtime, it wasn’t.

Imperial Japan, June 1942

The war began six months before on 7 December 1941 with a surprise Japanese attack on the U.S. Navy base at Pearl Harbor on Oahu in the Hawaiian Islands. The Imperial Japanese Navy’s First Air Fleet Kido Butai put the U.S. Navy’s Pacific Fleet battleline on the harbor bottom, announcing with convincing violence that a modern naval air arm trumped the traditional big-gun battleships of the world’s navies. In one of those ironies that litter military history, this demonstration of the effectiveness of naval air power came while the U.S. fleet’s carriers were safely away from Pearl.

While America scrambled to assemble men and matériel to fight this war, Thailand, Malaya, Guam, Wake Island and the Philippines fell to Japanese invaders. Imperial Japan now threatened Australia and had built up a strong protective screen for their Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere. Should Hawaii fall, the Pacific Ocean might as well be a Japanese lake.

Japanese soldiers, sailors and airmen came to believe themselves invincible. Much like today’s radical mujahideen, they believed that Americans were spoiled and weak and lacked the will to effectively respond.

In April, a bright spot emerged for the U.S. and her allies. A Pacific Fleet task force, including one of the aircraft carriers that the Pearl Harbor attack missed, snuck close to the Japanese home islands and launched sixteen Army B-25 “Mitchell” medium bombers against Tokyo and other Japanese cities—the Doolittle Raid. Little real damage was done, but the raid accomplished its primary goal of raising American morale at a time when it was at its lowest. More, it embarassed and enraged Japanese military leaders!

Imperial Navy Combined Fleet Admiral Isoroku Yamamoto knew he must complete the job begun at Pearl Harbor—he must finish off the U.S. Pacific Fleet by sinking its aircraft carriers. U.S. bombs falling in Tokyo, however ineffective, gave him just what he needed to sell a plan to the Naval General Staff in Tokyo: attack the U.S. Naval Air Facility on tiny Midway Atoll at the northwestern end of the Hawaiian Islands chain, about two-thirds of the way from Tokyo to Honolulu. Surely this would force the Pacific Fleet’s carriers at Pearl Harbor (about 1100 nautical miles from Midway) out into the open where they could be destroyed!

As the details of Yamamoto's plan were worked out, U.S. Navy codebreakers in Hawaii began to decode Japanese radio traffic concerning an objective for early June called “AF.” Suspecting that this might refer to Midway, Pacific Fleet Commander in Chief Admiral Chester Nimitz instructed the commander there to broadcast an uncoded message indicating that the base’s water purifiers had failed. The Japanese took the bait: they radioed instructions to load additional desalination equipment for AF. Now Nimitz knew where and when the attack would come.

On 27 May, about 75 ships of Yamamoto's Combined Fleet steamed from port in Japan bound for Midway, led by Kido Butai.
