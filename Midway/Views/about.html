The Game

Midway is is a two-player, turn-based, double-blind World War II historical simulation wargame. A simple one, as these things go. It's based on a landmark battle that occurred in early June, 1942 between forces of the United States Navy (USN) Pacific Fleet and the Imperial Japanese Navy (IJN) Combined Fleet. It will give players an idea of the challenges that faced the commanders of the opposing fleets but without so much detail that it is difficult to learn to play. This is a balancing act that results in some historical elements being overlooked—for example, destroyers and submarines have been left out of the game.

The game system is not particularly original; it's been tweaked, but veteran board wargamers will recognize it immediately.

Midway Atoll in November 1941

Each player takes a side: Japan’s forces commanded by Admiral Isoroku Yamamoto, or those of the United States under the command of Admiral Chester Nimitz. Players direct the movement and combat of the ships and aircraft actually present for the battle (except, of course, those destroyers and submarines mentioned above).

At first glance, the game might appear to be a sophisticated version of Battleship, and that’s not far wrong. Most of the game is played on a map of the central Pacific around Midway Atoll on which each player sees only their own ships and aircraft. The map has a square grid (about 20 miles to a side) that’s used to control movement and determine range. Players have a limited number of searches available each turn that they use by selecting grid locations. When enemy ships are located, only their types—battleship, aircraft carrier, light aircraft carrier, cruiser or light cruiser—are revealed. No infomation on the number of each type present is given, nor anything about any specific ship.

Beginning at 0500 on 3 June 1942, the game is played in turns representing two hours in daylight and five hours at night. Time runs out 34 turns later at 1700 on 6 June. The game ends when this last turn is completed or when either player chooses to retire. The winner is determined by points given for control of Midway Atoll and the destruction of enemy ships.

Each turn is divided into phases in which players move their ships, search for their opponent, and plan and execute combat. Combat is of two types: air or surface. Usually it’s air operations attack missions: sending aircraft that are available and in range to attack a grid square containing enemy ships. Ship-to-ship surface combat can occur when ships of both players wind up sharing one square on the map grid, but this is unusual (and never happened during the actual battle).

Starting a Game

On the Home page, players are presented with a list of the games that they are participating in. Select one and press the Play button to pick it up where it was left off.

To start a new game, select the “New Game” entry for the side you’d like to play. If there's a specific opponent you’r looking for, enter their nickname in the “Optional opponent nickname” text box. If you don’t enter a nickname, the first player seeking a new game on the other side will become your opponent. Click on the Play button to create the game and begin play. Once a new game is created, it is added to the list of available games and will appear there until completed.

Press the Play button and you’ll arrive on the Search Map.

Game Play

Midway is played in turns, and each turn consists of a series of phases with specific actions taken by each player. These phases are:

1. Movement
In this phase there are three things to do: bring newly-arrived ships into the game, move ships, and make aircraft ready for use. Do these in any order you choose.

The USN player gets all the ships he or she will ever have on the first turn; the IJN player receives reinforcing ships on 5 different turns during the game. New arrivals are displayed on the Arrivals tab. Click to select one or more, then click on one of the highlighted squares (aka “zones”) on the map edge—west (left) for the IJN player and east (right) for the USN—to place them there.

There is no limit to the number of ships that may occupy one zone. It is smart to keep your ships together in one or more groups; combat will demonstrate that your valuable aircraft carriers won’t last long unless supported by other ships.

Ships on the map, including new arrivals, can be moved up to two zones (six for the USN player on the first turn). To move ships, select the zone they’re in, select them on the Zone tab, then click on any highlighted zone to move them there. If you double-click on a zone, all your ships in that zone will be automatically selected.

Movement is optional, but new arrivals must be placed on the map.

If you wish, you may move your ships off the map: USN player via the east (right) map edge; IJN player off of the north (top), west (left) or south (bottom) edges. Ships moved off the map cannot return to the game.

Make your aircraft ready for use by clicking on the zone containing your aircraft carrier(s) or airbase (Midway), then clicking on the ready state icon on the aircraft carrier or airbase in the zone tab’s list. Aircraft in a “not ready” state (below the flight deck, shown) will become “readying,” and in the following turn will be ready for use. If you click on a ship’s “readying” or “ready” icon, you will knock it back to “not ready.” Your opponent is informed in the next phase when you ready or de-ready aircraft. Ships with readying or ready aircraft aboard are more easily sunk (more on that later).

End the phase by clicking the check mark button on the page“s upper right.

Victory

During the game points are awarded to the players as follows:

1. Starting with the 19th turn (0500 5 June) the U.S. player gets one point for each turn in which Midway is not taken by the Japanese player, for a possible total of 15.

Sunk, Enterprise is worth 10 points to the IJN player

2. The Japanese player receives 15 points for taking Midway before the game ends.

3. Ships are assigned point values, and players receive them when they sink an opponent’s ship. These point values are shown circled on ship images on the Search Map Arrivals and Zone tabs, and on the Battle Map.

When the game ends, the player with the most points has won!

The History

Background

On the morning of 4 June 1942, Imperial Japan was winning its war with the United States. By lunchtime, it wasn’t.

Imperial Japan, June 1942

The war began six months before on 7 December 1941 with a surprise Japanese attack on the U.S. Navy base at Pearl Harbor on Oahu in the Hawaiian Islands. The Imperial Japanese Navy’s First Air Fleet Kido Butai put the U.S. Navy’s Pacific Fleet battleline on the harbor bottom, announcing with convincing violence that a modern naval air arm trumped the traditional big-gun battleships of the world’s navies. In one of those ironies that litter military history, this demonstration of the effectiveness of naval air power came while the U.S. fleet’s carriers were safely away from Pearl.

While America scrambled to assemble men and matériel to fight this war, Thailand, Malaya, Hong Kong, Guam, Wake Island and the Philippines fell to Japanese invaders. Imperial Japan now threatened Australia and had built up a strong protective screen for their Greater East Asia Co-Prosperity Sphere. Should Hawaii fall, the Pacific Ocean might as well be a Japanese lake.

Japanese soldiers, sailors and airmen came to believe themselves invincible. Much like today’s radical mujahideen, they believed that Americans were spoiled and weak and lacked the will to fight.

In April, a bright spot emerged for the U.S. and her allies. A Pacific Fleet task force, including one of the aircraft carriers that the Pearl Harbor attack missed, snuck close to the Japanese home islands and launched sixteen Army B-25 “Mitchell” medium bombers against Tokyo and other Japanese cities—the Doolittle Raid. Little real damage was done, but the raid accomplished its primary goal of raising American morale at a time when it was at its lowest. More, it embarassed and enraged Japan’s military leadership.

Imperial Navy Combined Fleet Admiral Isoroku Yamamoto knew he must complete the job begun at Pearl Harbor—he must finish off the U.S. Pacific Fleet by sinking its aircraft carriers. U.S. bombs falling in Tokyo, however ineffective, gave him just what he needed to sell a plan to the Naval General Staff in Tokyo: attack the U.S. Naval Air Facility on tiny Midway Atoll at the northwestern end of the Hawaiian Islands chain, about two-thirds of the way from Tokyo to Honolulu. Surely this would force the Pacific Fleet’s carriers at Pearl Harbor (about 1100 nautical miles from Midway) out into the open where they could be destroyed!

As the Imperial Navy planners worked out the details of Yamamoto’s plan, a group of U.S. Navy codebreakers in Hawaii began to decode Japanese radio traffic concerning an objective for early June called “AF.” Suspecting that this might refer to Midway, Pacific Fleet Commander in Chief Admiral Chester Nimitz instructed the commander there to broadcast an uncoded message indicating that the base’s water purifiers had failed. The Japanese took the bait: they radioed instructions to load additional desalination equipment for “AF.” Now Nimitz knew where and when the attack would come.

On 27 May, ships of Yamamoto’s Combined Fleet steamed from port in Japan bound for Midway, led by Kido Butai with four large aircraft carriers and 229 warplanes. By 2 June, three carriers with about 234 aircraft and supporting ships of Nimitz’s Pacific Fleet lay in wait about 350 miles northwest of Midway.
