The Game

Midway is is a two-player, turn-based, double-blind World War II historical simulation wargame. It is based on a landmark historical battle that began on 3 June 1942 between forces of the United States Navy Pacific Fleet and the Imperial Japanese Navy Combined Fleet. It will give players an idea of the challenges facing the commanders of the opposing fleets, but without so much detail that it is difficult to learn to play. This is a balancing act that results in some historical elements being overlooked—for example, destroyers and submarines have been left out. The game system is not particularly original; it's been tweaked, but veteran board wargamers will recognize it immediately. Any changes we’ve made were in an effort to take better advantage of this medium without losing too much of its old-time map-and-counters feel.

Midway Atoll in November 1941

Each player takes a side: Japan’s forces commanded by Admiral Isoroku Yamamoto, or those of the United States under the overall command of Admiral Chester Nimitz. Players direct the movement and combat of the ships and aircraft actually present for the battle. The historical object is control of Midway Atoll. It begins the game in U.S. hands and can pay big dividends to the Japanese player if he or she can successfully capture it.

The game begins at 0500 3 June 1942 and is played in turns representing two hours in daylight and five hours at night. At 1700 on 6 June—34 turns later—time runs out. The game ends when the last turn is completed, when one player withdraws all of his or her ships from the map, or when a player chooses to retire. The winner is determined by points given for control of Midway and the destruction of enemy ships.

At first glance, the game might appear to be a more sophisticated version of Battleship, and that’s not far wrong. Most of the game is played on a map of the central Pacific around Midway Atoll where neither player can see the other’s ships. The map has a square grid that’s used to control movement and determine range (each square scales to about 20 miles across). Players have a limited number of searches each turn that they use by selecting grid locations. When enemy ships are located they may be attacked.

Combat occurs in two ways. Usually it’s one player launching planes from carriers to attack his or her opponent from the air. Should ships of both players wind up sharing one square on the map grid, ship-to-ship surface combat may ensue, but this is quite unusual (and never happened historically).

The History

Background

On the morning of 4 June 1942, Imperial Japan was winning its new war with the United States. By nightfall, that was no longer true.

Imperial Japan, 1942

The war began six months before in December, 1941 with a Japanese surprise attack on the U.S. Navy base at Pearl Harbor on Oahu in the Hawaiian Islands. The Imperial Japanese Navy’s aircraft carrier striking force Kido Butai launched about 350 warplanes that sank 21 ships of the U.S. Navy’s Pacific Fleet, announcing with convincing violence that a modern naval air arm trumped the traditional big-gun battleships of the world’s navies. In one of those ironies that litter military history, the IJN’s carrier-based attack came while the U.S. fleet’s carriers and escorts were safely away from Pearl.
